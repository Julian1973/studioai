# The Adventure Begins — Gate 1 Storyboard

**Status: DRAFT for Julian's review. Nothing in this document is signed, fired, or rendered.**

- Episode: 1
- Logline: Keen sails away from home to prove he is ready, loses his way in a storm, and discovers that the love he carries becomes courage when a friend needs him.
- Theme: Growing up means stepping into the storm with fear still inside you, and discovering that love can become courage when you carry it forward.
- Scenes: 10 · Beats: 46

**Lead arc:**
> Keen begins wanting to prove he is ready, capable, and brave on his own; underneath, he needs to understand that real courage is not fearlessness or independence, but acting through fear while accepting that love, family, and help are still with him.

**Story spine:**
> Once upon a time, in Crystal Cove, there was a young bear named Keen who wanted to begin his own adventure and prove he was ready. Every day, he tried to be brave by looking certain, checking everything twice, and saying, “I’ve got this.” Until one day, his Mum gave him his father’s wristbands and let him sail away from home. Because of that, Keen crossed open water, met Squeaky, and was caught in a storm that took his map and left him lost. Because of that, he followed Squeaky toward a mysterious light and found that his new friend was trapped and sinking. Until finally, Keen chose courage before he had time to look brave, dove into the storm, saved Squeaky, and discovered the wristbands glowing with the love he carried. And ever since then, Keen has belonged to Crystal Cove not because he never gets scared, but because he knows bravery can have a gulp in it.

**Promise:**
> By the final frame, the audience must feel that Keen has found a new home without losing the old one — the beach is warmer, the cove is safer, and the adventure ahead feels possible because he is loved.

## North Star — the six questions, answered for this episode

> These six questions are my own synthesis (not an established, itemized "six" found anywhere in canon) — CRYSTAL_BEARS_LOCKED_CANON.md §0 only states four named test questions plus eight separately-numbered craft laws, so this framing is provisional pending Julian's actual definition of "the six."

NOTE ON SOURCING: Canon (§0 NORTH STAR) states four test questions and eight separately-numbered craft laws — it does not itemize a "six questions" set anywhere. The six below are my own synthesis, built by merging the four test questions with the most load-bearing craft laws (2 Want/Need, 3 Surrender, 4 wordless held beat, 6 catch-and-release, 7 bittersweet close) into a single usable per-episode checklist. 1) WHERE IS THE FEELING MADE VISIBLE RATHER THAN NARRATED?
Twice, both multisensory and wordless-first:
— Keen's father's WRISTBANDS begin a faint self-glow underwater in Scene 7 while he strains against the drift net ("A faint glow begins inside them. Soft. Steady.") — courage rendered as light on the body, not a line of dialogue about being brave. Canon's EP3 production notes confirm this is deliberately NOT crystal-powered yet — "courage from within, not the crystal."
— The Crystal Bears' combined beacon in Scene 6: Aida's Rose Quartz, then Misty's, Luna's, Howey's, Zenny's crystals pulse one by one, then "A brilliant BURST OF COMBINED LIGHT shoots out across the storm-dark sea" — collective care and hope made into a literal, visible-from-the-boat signal Keen can see and react to ("What is that…?").
— And the culminating instance: Scene 9's Aquamarine Crystal materializing, splitting into two gems, and settling into the (previously vacant) wristbands with "a pulse of AZURE LIGHT" Keen "feels it instantly" — the show's one true invention (feeling on the outside) landing exactly on the earned-crystal beat.

2) WHAT IS THE WANT VS THE NEED, EPISODE-WIDE? (craft law 2)
WANT (stated, performed, on the surface): Keen wants to prove he can do this alone — "I've got this, Mum" (Sc.3), "I've got this… I've got this…" repeated to himself mid-storm (Sc.4) and again bracing against the net (Sc.7), plus his running performance of confidence ("Still got it!" after nearly capsizing; the map-reading bravado he immediately fumbles). The WANT is competence performed solo — independence as self-sufficiency.
NEED (what the crystal/story actually resolves): Keen needs to learn that courage isn't going it alone — it's "part of growing up" that still means never being alone. His Mum names the real need twice before he can access it: "Wherever you go… you'll never be alone" (Sc.3) and Aida closes the loop verbatim in Sc.9: "That's what family does… Wherever you go, you'll never be alone." The gap between the WANT (I can do this by myself) and the NEED (courage is choosing to act FOR someone else, inside a net of people who have your back) is exactly where the dive for Squeaky lives — an unhesitating act of selflessness, not solo heroics, is what the crystal actually rewards.

3) WHERE IS THE ONE WORDLESS HELD BEAT (THE NADIR)? (craft law 4)
Scene 4, the moment the map is snatched by the wind and vanishes into the storm: "Keen stands frozen. Rainwater sloshes around his feet. The horizon disappears behind sheets of grey." This is the beat with no plan, no ally in frame (Squeaky has vanished beneath the water), and no crystal light yet — just Keen alone, small, and truly lost, immediately before "Oh no… I'm lost!!!" This is the true nadir: total disorientation, held a beat longer than comfortable, camera on his face in the downpour, before he has to self-soothe his way back ("I've got this… I've got this…"). It is the emotional low of the whole episode — the point every subsequent beat (Squeaky's rescue signal, the beacon, the dive, the crystal) resolves.

4) WHERE IS THE SURRENDER BEAT (NOT A POWER-UP)? (craft law 3)
Scene 7, underwater: Keen has already tried and failed to free Squeaky ("He grabs the net and pulls. Nothing… Still nothing. His eyes flick upward… He is running out of strength"). The turn is NOT a power surge — it's the moment he stops trying to force it alone and instead centers himself and simply commits fully: "He plants his feet against a submerged rock. Centers himself… I've got this… I've got this…" followed by the quiet escalation to "COME ON!" and the net finally tearing. Canon is explicit that this is deliberately pre-crystal (the wristbands are still "vacant," only self-glowing from courage, not crystal power) — the surrender is Keen letting the feeling (fear, exhaustion, the urge to save himself first) go and choosing Squeaky anyway, sincerity not volume. The ACTUAL Crystal Call/power-up doesn't fire until Scene 9, safely on shore, well AFTER the feeling has already been felt and acted on — exactly matching craft law 3's warning that a Call placed before the feeling is felt is the single biggest soul-violation. Here the order is correctly feeling → surrender → (much later) crystal.

5) WHERE IS THE CATCH-AND-RELEASE (FUNNY GOES QUIETLY TRUE, THEN EXITS WITHIN ~2S)? (craft law 6)
Scene 1: Fuzzby's comic pollen-moustache bit ("Do I look official?" / Zenny: "Yes Fuzzby. Officially nuts!") is pure gag-and-move-on — but the beat right after quietly turns true for half a second: a distant thunder RUMBLE, Fuzzby pauses mid-hover and says "…okay that sounded dramatic," Zenny looks to the sky and says, simply, "A Storm's coming" — a flicker of real unease inside the comedy — and it's released almost instantly: "Good thing I work well under pressure" / he immediately flies into a flower and gets stuck again, back to slapstick within roughly two lines. The lump (something's coming) and the laugh (he's stuck in a flower again) sit in the same breath, then the scene moves on without dwelling.
A second, later instance: Scene 9, Fuzzby "claps too early. Stops. Then starts clapping again louder to cover it" during Keen's Crystal Call — a half-second of vulnerable, true-to-character overeagerness inside the triumphant beat, immediately swallowed back into the group hug and comedy of Scene 10's honey riff.

6) WHERE IS THE BITTERSWEET CLOSE (THE ACHE RETAINED, NEVER RESET TO ZERO)? (craft law 7)
The episode's ending (Scene 10, the beach walk with the honey-riff comedy) is warm and light, but the ache it carries forward is Keen's father — never resolved away, only carried. The wristbands stay on Keen's wrists through the entire final act, explicitly named as continuity of loss and love: "I still feel him… every day," "Promise me you'll always wear them," and Keen's own "I won't be [alone]. I love you, Mum." By Scene 9 those same wristbands (once vacant, a physical reminder of an absence) now hold the Aquamarine crystal — the show doesn't erase the father's absence, it lets the crystal settle INTO the object that already carried that grief, so the joyful ending is warmer, but the trace of what it cost (a boy without his father, wearing his father's bands into every scene from here on) is still visibly on his body in the episode's very last images. It lands as an addition of belonging (the Bears, the crystal, the friendship with Fuzzby/Zenny) layered over an ache that is retained, not zeroed out — precisely the show's "never resolve the ache away" rule.
